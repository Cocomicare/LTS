Clinical Reference · From Cocomi's Care Team
🌡 Call the Team Immediately If
  • Fever over 100°F — always the first sign of either rejection or infection
  • Cough that is new, worsening, or producing thick, dark, or foul-smelling sputum
  • Shortness of breath or falling O₂ saturation
  • Spirometry reading drops 10% or more from personal baseline
  • Chest pain, swelling in legs, or sudden weight gain
🔬 Signs of Rejection
  • Shortness of breath, decreased spirometry readings
  • Fever — may be absent with heavy immunosuppression
  • "Flu-like" symptoms or minor changes in how breathing feels
  • Pain over the incision site
  • Note: rejection signs can be very subtle — spirometry catches it early
🦠 Signs of Infection
  • Fever, chills, fatigue, malaise
  • Change in sputum color or smell
  • Increased cough frequency or effort
  • Key pathogens to know: CMV (cytomegalovirus), PCP pneumonia, Aspergillus (fungal)
  • Immunosuppressed patients are highly vulnerable — act fast, don't wait
📉 Watch: Magnesium Levels
  • Anti-rejection drugs (tacrolimus) cause dangerous magnesium loss
  • Low mag symptoms: irregular heartbeat, muscle weakness, irritability, seizures
  • Low mag raises infection risk AND cardiovascular risk
  • Ask the team about Cocomi's magnesium at every check-in
  • Dietary sources: green leafy vegetables, nuts, beans, seeds
💊 Anti-Rejection Drugs (Immunosuppressants)
  • Tacrolimus (FK506) — calcineurin inhibitor, the gold standard. Blood levels checked every 12 hours initially, then after labs. Never miss a dose.
  • Mycophenolate mofetil (CellCept) — taken precisely 12 hours apart, often after lab tests
  • Prednisone/Steroids — take with food at the same time daily. Stress taking them with food (check specific drug)
  • Extended-release tacrolimus (Astagraf XL, Envarsus XR) — once-daily; do not crush or chew
🛡 Antimicrobial Prophylaxis Drugs
  • Valganciclovir — prevents CMV (cytomegalovirus), a major post-transplant threat
  • TMP-SMX (Bactrim) — prevents PCP pneumonia
  • Fluconazole / Voriconazole — antifungal coverage; voriconazole for higher-risk periods
  • As immunosuppression dose rises, more of these drugs are added — this is the antimicrobial drug toxicity the gauges show
⏰ Medication Timing Rules
  • Tacrolimus and mycophenolate: exactly 12 hours apart
  • Steroids: same time daily, always with food
  • Non-adherence is a major cause of late rejection
  • Blood trough levels are checked regularly — these tell the team if the dose is right
  • Never adjust doses on your own — always contact the team first
🚫 Critical Avoids
  • Grapefruit & grapefruit juice — dangerously increases tacrolimus levels in the blood
  • Pomegranate, starfruit, Seville oranges — same effect
  • Herbal supplements — many interact with immunosuppressants (St. John's Wort especially)
  • Live vaccines — contraindicated; consult the transplant team before any vaccine
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — can worsen kidney toxicity from calcineurin inhibitors
🦴 Bone & Metabolic Effects (Anti-Rejection Drugs)
  • Osteoporosis — 5× increased fracture risk in transplant recipients. DXA scan and spine x-rays at 3 and 6 months post-transplant
  • Bone loss occurs most rapidly in the first 3–6 months
  • Diabetes — hyperglycemia very common post-transplant; monitor glucose. Screen at 45 days post-transplant
  • Skin thinning & fragility — use high-SPF sunscreen daily; increased skin cancer risk
🫘 Kidney & Blood Pressure Effects
  • Nephrotoxicity — tacrolimus (calcineurin inhibitors) can damage kidneys over time; creatinine monitored closely
  • Hypertension — reported in 50–80% of solid organ transplant patients post-transplant
  • Target blood pressure: 130/80 mmHg
  • Hypertension + tacrolimus together accelerate kidney decline — both must be managed
⚗ Antimicrobial Drug Burden
  • Voriconazole and valganciclovir add significant liver and kidney load
  • EBV-positive PTLD (lymphoma) is a known complication of intensive immunosuppression
  • CMV infection itself damages the transplanted lung and increases rejection risk
  • The team weighs: how much infection prophylaxis is needed vs how much toxicity can be tolerated — this is the antimicrobial gauge
🧠 Neurological & Other Effects
  • Tacrolimus can cause tremors, headaches, and nerve sensitivity
  • Low magnesium (from tacrolimus) worsens neurological symptoms
  • Steroids can cause mood changes, insomnia, and weight gain
  • Low magnesium impairs insulin secretion → raises diabetes risk further
  • NK cell proliferation suppressed by low magnesium → 15% higher severe infection risk
🫁 The Denervated Lung — Critical Concept
  • The new lung has no nerve connection to the brain
  • Cocomi will not feel the urge to cough the way a normal lung signals it
  • She will not feel the urge to breathe deeply as a reflex
  • Deep breathing and coughing must be deliberate, conscious habits — not reflexes
  • She must be instructed to cough voluntarily every 1–2 hours to clear secretions
📊 Spirometry — The Early Warning System
  • Daily or twice-daily home spirometry is required to monitor for chronic rejection (BOS/CLAD)
  • Spirometry catches early signs of rejection before symptoms appear
  • A drop of 10% or more from personal baseline = call the team
  • Consistent daily readings build a personal baseline — consistency matters more than any single number
😮‍💨 Secretion Management
  • Watch for thick, dark, or foul-smelling sputum — this indicates infection
  • Forced Expiratory Volume (FEV) spirometry helps monitor for clearance
  • Airway complications (primary graft dysfunction) can present as severe hypoxemia in first 72 hours
  • Daily mobilization and early ambulation help prevent secretion buildup
🩸 Clot Risk (VTE)
  • Post-transplant patients are at high risk for blood clots (venous thromboembolism)
  • Ensure early ambulation — movement is protective
  • Anticoagulants like Enoxaparin may be used; monitor blood counts
  • Report leg swelling, chest pain, or sudden shortness of breath immediately
🏠 Daily Home Priorities
  • Spirometry twice daily — log every reading, watch for downward trends
  • Temperature check daily — fever over 100°F means call immediately
  • Weight daily — sudden gain can signal fluid retention or organ stress
  • Medications exactly on schedule — tacrolimus and mycophenolate 12 hours apart, steroids with food
  • Blood pressure — target below 130/80; track and report changes
🧼 Infection Prevention at Home
  • Strict hand hygiene — the single most effective protection
  • Mask in public — no exceptions during high-risk period
  • No fresh flowers or plants in the room — soil and pollen carry fungal spores
  • Avoid sick visitors — even a cold is dangerous with suppressed immunity
  • CHG bathing, peri-care routines as directed by the care team
  • No raw or unwashed foods — high-SPF foods diet strictly followed
☀️ Skin & Cancer Vigilance
  • Immunosuppression increases skin cancer risk significantly
  • Apply high-SPF sunscreen daily, even on cloudy days
  • Avoid prolonged sun exposure
  • Report any new or changing skin lesions to the team
  • Annual dermatology screening recommended long-term
🥗 Diet & Lifestyle
  • No grapefruit, pomegranate, or starfruit — ever
  • Magnesium-rich foods: green leafy vegetables, nuts, beans, seeds
  • No raw or undercooked meats, unpasteurized dairy, or unwashed produce
  • Glucose monitoring if hyperglycemia develops — diet matters for steroid-induced diabetes
  • Adequate hydration supports kidney function under tacrolimus load
